This project explores decentralised thought through fungi, networks, and time. Timed to a 7-minute narration, each visual shift reflects a new perspective on multispecies intelligence.

'Technology often presupposes human domination, but it could instead reflect our ecological dependence"

[0:00–0:252]

It’s lonely at the top, but it doesn’t have to be...

[0:3.14 – 0:15.90]

[lonely at the top]

We humans often see ourselves as the anointed objects of evolution, our intelligence representing the leading edge of unlikely order in an entropic universe.

[00:12–02:52]
[0:17–0:23]

While there’s no way to determine any purpose behind what produced us, that hasn’t stopped some from making assertions.

[0:23–0:45]

Consider the school of thought called longtermism. This worldview — held by influential people like Elon Musk, Peter Thiel, and others — sees the prime directive of Homo sapiens as maximizing our species' “potential.” often defined in utilitarian terms: population, distribution, comfort over millennia.

[0:45–1:00]

Its extreme version imagines an interstellar destiny — the stars as substrates for extending our species’ gifts. Some envision our descendants living in virtual bliss, the matrix as utopia.

[1:00–1:14]

Longtermism overlaps with transhumanism.Philosopher Nick Bostrom calls humanity “a half-baked beginning we can learn to remold.” Humans are not an end, but a means, becoming.

More than human like Tyrell in Blade Runner. “More human than human is our motto.”

[1:21–1:34]

Rather than deepening our role in the world, these visions consummate a long process of separation enabled by technology and capital. But that’s not the only conception of the More-than-human.

[1:34–1:52]

In Ways of Being, James Bridle reframes it as a mega-category:
microbes, plants, water, stone, even machines
— a grouping so vast it becomes undefinable. And that’s the point. Interaction is what matters.

[1:53–2:07]

,This usage dismisses human exceptionalism and recognizes our ecological existence co-constructed with the world. Human intelligence is just one "flowering” of a universal phenomenon.
As Bridle writes,

"Intelligence is not something which exists, but something one does. It is active, interpersonal, generative."

[2:15–2:33]

Mind and meaning arise through relationship. Elevating human agency above all else makes little sense. Our minds, like our bodies, are contingent on everything else. The path forward is to move with the world, not escape or surpass it.

[2:33–2:45]

This thinking borrows from Indigenous cosmologies. It de-centers human priorities within an infinite network of agents. No viewpoint is favored — not even biological over mechanical.

[2:46–2:58]
[2:46–2:58]

We are invited to engage the more than human world as fellow subjects, not objects, challenging the impulse to conquer nature reinforced by colonialist sciences

[2:59–3:07]

Even Darwin, often cited for competition, acknowledged that evolution involves collaboration as much as struggle.

[3:07–3:26]

Technology is also part of the more than human, but today, it mostly serves capital, reinforcing human dominance. It gave humans massive advantages. We didn’t rise due to strength or speed. And now, some of the most powerful beings are technologists.

[3:26–3:48]

Musk dreams of escaping Earth. Bezos imagines a planet walled off from humans. This reveals a deeper alienation from the Earth that made us. This impulse extends to eliminating death, a vital part of life. To transcend death would also mean to transcend life

— becoming something no longer human.

[3:50–4:06]

Capitalism depends on dualism — mind separate from biological matter. Anthropologist Jason Hickel notes this separateness-enabled ecosystem exploitation. Capitalism had to destroy the worldview of interdependence and replace it

Tech under capitalism has deepened that separation, increasing disconnection from nature. Though tech connects in some ways, we face social, ecological, and psychological alienation.
[4:06–4:20]

Tech under capitalism has deepened that separation,increasing disconnection from nature, Though tech connects in some ways, we face social, ecological, and psychological alienation.

[04:19–04:24]
People talk of escaping to nature, as if nature isn’t everywhere.
[4:25–4:41]

Meanwhile, technology offers chances to rethink agency and personhood.

Artificial intelligence, may also expand our ideas of sentience. Debates don’t hinge on whether machines are truly minds, but whether we’ll treat them as such.

Bridle sees this as a chance to de-center ourselves as arbiters of meaning. That could be tech’s highest calling not domination, but humility.

[4:53–5:22]

Bridle joins thinkers like Donna Haraway, Octavia Butler, and Adrienne Maree Brown in calling for reciprocity, not hierarchy. Popular interest in fungi, foraging, tarot, and traditional knowledge reflects a curiosity about our role in nature. Works like Everything Everywhere All at Once and An Immense World echo this impulse to rethink connection.

[5:22–5:36]

Maybe these are signs of rejecting human chauvinism a mindset that’s led to despair and ecological collapse. New tools and perspectives might open frontiers to exploit — or opportunities for humility.

[5:36–6:09]

If there’s a perfect opposite to the dream of human empires in space, it’s wandering the woods. Foraging means listening and responding to nature. A hunch about a mushroom might be right — as if a thought is shared with the woods.

Ethnographer Anna Tsing notes that wild mushrooms press us into multi-species ecologies where control is impossible. Mycologist Patty Kaishian describes her work as timed wandering — guided by intuition, not rigor, but relationship.

[6:10–6:19]

Science barely understands these subtle interactions, but stories and ritual have helped people engage with them for millennia.

As Bridle says, exploring randomly may be the best way to learn — letting the world think with us.

[6:25–6:38]

If our minds are co-constructed with the world,
can any intelligence be called artificial?

Fungi are now known to be smarter than once believed.
But ask a forager, and they’ll say they already knew.

[6:38–6:49]

My own reconnection to nature began on a mountaintop in San Francisco taking psychedelic mushrooms. I felt fellowship with shrubs, the city, the sky — a deep sense of being part of a majestic whole.

[6:49–7:07]

These mushrooms are called technology by some, but the relationship between our minds and psilocybin is emergent and mysterious. Their mind-altering effects remind us of the contingent nature of our brains, shaped by the world we share.

[7:07–7:20]

As Tsing writes, “Mushrooms grow in the seams of empire. They emerge through cracks, offering alternatives to the paved-over world of capitalism.”

[7:20–7:30]

They can’t speak, but that doesn’t mean we shouldn’t listen. While technologists look to the stars for escape, maybe the way out of the woods is finding our way back into them.
